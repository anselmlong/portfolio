# Writing Style Analysis - Anselm Long

## Tone & Voice
- **Casual and conversational**: "Hey everyone", "What dumb project did I do again?", "Lowkey, I seem to have a knack for useless projects", "Alright"
- **Self-deprecating humor**: Admits mistakes openly, uses phrases like "useless side project"
- **Honest about struggles**: "This was a fun but very painful project", "I kept running into out of memory errors"
- **Relatable frustration**: "aw hell nah i ain't waiting 38 hours", "Nooooo..."
- **Enthusiastic when things work**: "THIS ACTUALLY SOUNDS LEGIT-ish!!!", "Such a mood. Wow. This is me."
- **Accepts limitations**: "I've kinda resigned to my fate that my laptop is just slow"
- **Casual dismissals**: "(don't ask why it's named that way)", "duh!"
- **Data-driven but casual**: Can present statistics and analysis while maintaining conversational tone
- **Observational**: "ChatSelm is eerily similar to me"

## Structure Patterns
- **TL;DR section** at the top for quick summary (when applicable)
- **Visual-first opening**: Video or image at the top with caption
- **Numbered/sequential steps**: "First Step", "Second", "Third", etc. OR just "1.", "2.", "3."
- **Iterative documentation**: "First Run", "Second Run", "Third Run" showing the journey
- **Interactive elements**: "See if you can guess which is mine!", quizzes, answer keys
- **Data presentation**: Extensive use of tables for comparisons, results, statistics
- **Methodology sections**: Explains how experiments/evaluations were conducted
- **Answer keys**: Provided at the end for interactive content
- **Spoiler warnings**: "I'll spoil it in the next section anyway"
- **"Learning points!" section** at the end (alternative to "Reflections")
- **References section** with numbered list (when applicable)
- **Clear section headers** that are descriptive but casual, sometimes with uncertainty: "It Works... Generally!"
- **GitHub repo links** early in the post
- **Call to action**: "Let me know how many you get correct!"

## Language Patterns
- **Frequent contractions**: "I'm", "it's", "don't", "can't", "won't"
- **Casual interjections**: "lolz", "HAHAHAHAAA", "aw hell nah", "duh!"
- **Opening phrases**: "Lowkey", "Hey everyone", "What dumb project did I do again?", "Alright"
- **Transitional phrases**: "But without Further Ado", "Great - now I had my data", "At this point", "Eventually", "Needless to say", "In the end"
- **Sentence starters**: "So!", "Great -", "Unfortunately", "Now,", "Out of the box"
- **Parenthetical asides**: Frequent use of parentheses for clarifications and side thoughts: "(don't ask why it's named that way)", "(duh!)", "(i.e. I'm in Singapore)"
- **Rhetorical questions**: Used sparingly but effectively
- **Casual qualifiers**: "kinda", "lowkey", "honestly", "I think"
- **Accepting limitations**: "I've kinda resigned to my fate"
- **Statistical language**: "28.2% (~30%)", "over an assumed baseline of 0%", "Average (close friends): 7.75 / 10"
- **Observational statements**: "ChatSelm is eerily similar to me"

## Technical Writing Approach
- **Accessible explanations**: Breaks down complex concepts (QLoRA, Axolotl, ViT) in simple terms
- **Shows the process**: Documents failures, iterations, and debugging
- **Includes actual code/config**: Full code blocks with explanations
- **Explains "why"**: Not just what was done, but why choices were made
- **Shows errors**: Includes actual error messages and how they were solved
- **Before/after comparisons**: Shows progression through iterations
- **Cross-references other work**: "I used it in [freakcha]" - links to previous projects
- **Notes about environment**: "Note to anyone who's trying this in a Linux environment..."
- **Surprised by results**: "I was honestly impressed", "worked surprisingly well"

## Formatting Style
- **Bold for emphasis**: Key terms and important concepts, sometimes for entire phrases
- **Italics for emphasis**: Used for specific terms or casual emphasis ("_gesture recognition_")
- **Code blocks**: Extensive use with language tags and inline explanations
- **Blockquotes**: For definitions and explanations from sources, often multi-paragraph
- **Video content**: HTML video tags at the top with captions
- **Image references**: With descriptive alt text and captions, often multiple per post
- **Tables**: Extensive use for data presentation, comparisons, results (markdown tables with alignment)
- **Lists**: Both bulleted and numbered, used liberally, sometimes nested
- **Inline code**: Uses backticks for technical terms, file names, commands
- **Emojis**: Used sparingly in text (üòÆ‚Äçüí®, üò≠, ü§©) but more in examples
- **Figcaptions**: Used for all images/videos, often with personality: "Look how happy I am!", "My computer vision debut"
- **Answer keys**: Simple format like "AABBAAABBA" at the end
- **Sectioned data**: Groups related data (e.g., "Close friends" vs "Lesser-known friends")

## Narrative Style
- **Journey-focused**: Tells the story of the process, not just the result
- **Results-focused**: Can also present data-driven analysis and evaluations
- **Personal anecdotes**: Includes reactions, feelings, and personal context
- **Shows learning**: "I had no idea what any of the options meant", "I think learning vim was a pain in the ass but once you get used to it"
- **Conversational flow**: Natural transitions between topics
- **Balanced**: Shows both excitement and frustration equally
- **Methodology transparency**: Explains how experiments were set up and conducted
- **Honest about results**: "it performs well with short responses. Unfortunately, on longer responses it hallucinates"

## Technical Documentation Style
- **Config file explanations**: Shows full configs with line-by-line explanations
- **Command examples**: Shows actual commands used
- **Error documentation**: Includes actual error messages
- **Workflow descriptions**: Step-by-step processes clearly laid out
- **Troubleshooting sections**: Documents issues and solutions
- **Experimental methodology**: Explains how evaluations/experiments were set up
- **Data presentation**: Uses tables extensively for results, comparisons, statistics
- **Statistical analysis**: Includes averages, percentages, error tallies
- **Comparison tables**: Side-by-side comparisons (e.g., "My Response" vs "LLM's Response")

## Humor & Personality
- Self-aware about the absurdity of projects
- Uses humor to break tension ("aw hell nah", "Nooooo...")
- Includes funny/relatable moments in examples
- Shows personality through reactions and asides
- Doesn't take self too seriously

## Evaluation/Experiment Post Structure
- **Interactive opening**: "See if you can guess which is mine!"
- **Full quiz section**: Presents all prompts and responses upfront
- **Spoiler warnings**: "I'll spoil it in the next section anyway"
- **Comparison tables**: Side-by-side "My Response" vs "LLM's Response"
- **Methodology explanation**: How the evaluation was set up
- **Voting/results sections**: Organized by groups (e.g., "Close friends" vs "Lesser-known friends")
- **Statistical presentation**: Averages, percentages, error tallies in tables
- **Observational conclusions**: "ChatSelm is eerily similar to me"
- **Honest assessment**: Acknowledges both strengths and weaknesses
- **Answer key**: Simple format at the end (e.g., "AABBAAABBA")
- **Call to action**: "Let me know how many you get correct!"

## Key Takeaways for Replication
1. **Be conversational** - Write like you're explaining to a friend
2. **Show the struggle** - Don't hide failures, document them
3. **Explain simply** - Break down complex concepts
4. **Use code liberally** - Show, don't just tell
5. **Be honest** - Admit when you don't know something
6. **Show iterations** - Document the journey, not just the destination
7. **Mix formal and casual** - Technical accuracy with casual tone
8. **Include personal reactions** - "Such a mood. Wow.", "I was honestly impressed"
9. **Use specific phrases** - "But without Further Ado", "At this point", "Lowkey", "Alright", etc.
10. **Balance enthusiasm and frustration** - Show both sides authentically
11. **Start with visuals** - Video or compelling image at the top
12. **Accept limitations** - "I've kinda resigned to my fate"
13. **Cross-reference work** - Link to previous projects when relevant
14. **Use casual dismissals** - "(don't ask why)", "duh!" for obvious things
15. **End with learning points** - Either "Reflections" or "Some learning points!" format
16. **Use tables for data** - Extensive markdown tables for comparisons and results
17. **Include interactive elements** - Quizzes, answer keys, "see if you can guess"
18. **Present methodology** - Explain how experiments/evaluations were conducted
19. **Show statistical analysis** - Averages, percentages, error tallies when relevant
20. **Add call to action** - "Let me know how many you get correct!" at the end
